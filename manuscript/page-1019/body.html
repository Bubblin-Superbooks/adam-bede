“I'll send to Seth,” said Mr. Poyser. “I'll send Alick to tell him to come, or else to send up word o' the woman's name, an' thee canst write a letter ready to send off to Treddles'on as soon as we can make out a direction.”

“It's poor work writing letters when you want folks to come to you i' trouble,” said Mrs. Poyser. “Happen it'll be ever so long on the road, an' never reach her at last.”

Before Alick arrived with the message, Lisbeth's thoughts too had already flown to Dinah, and she had said to Seth, “Eh, there's no comfort for us i' this world any more, wi'out thee couldst get Dinah Morris to come to us, as she did when my old man died. I'd like her to come in an' take me by th' hand again, an' talk to me. She'd tell me the rights on't, belike—she'd happen know some good i' all this trouble an' heart-break comin' upo' that poor lad, as ne'er done a bit o' wrong
